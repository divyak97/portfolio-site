01
The Opportunity

Turning a business concept into a seller product

Poshmark sellers rely on the platform for real income. But the marketplace had become saturated, and the only free distribution lever — "sharing" — was losing its effectiveness. Sellers were frustrated, calling it "a gimmick and a time suck."

Every major competitor — eBay, Etsy, Mercari, Facebook Marketplace — already had a first-party ads product. Poshmark didn't. Discovery interviews with 11 sellers confirmed a clear willingness to pay for better visibility.

"You should charge money to have a marketing campaign."

— Etoya Makins, Pro Seller

The initial concept resembled traditional marketplace advertising: sellers selecting individual listings, choosing keywords, setting durations, managing campaigns. The business case was strong. But the design challenge quickly revealed itself.

The core design challenge

Poshmark sellers are not performance marketers.
Most are everyday people selling from their closets — not advertisers familiar with CPC models, campaign setup, or keyword targeting. How might we make advertising feel effortless for them?

Through discovery interviews and research, three distinct seller archetypes emerged — each with different motivations, habits, and pain points around visibility and promotion.

02
Defining the Product Strategy

The pivot that defined everything

My first explorations followed the market-standard pattern — a listing-level ad creation flow. Pick listings, set keywords, choose a duration, manage a budget per listing. Standard. Validated by competitors. Logical.

Then I presented it in a leadership review with the CEO. The feedback reframed the entire product:

Poshmark sellers aren't power advertisers. They're moms, students, side-hustlers. The experience needs to be as simple as they are.

This was the defining moment. Instead of asking sellers to make 10 decisions, we'd ask them to make one: a daily budget. Their entire closet would be promoted automatically based on buyer search relevance. They'd only pay per click.

Before — Sponsored Listings
Complex, marketer-first
  • Manually select listings
  • Choose keywords
  • Set duration per listing
  • Manage bid per item
  • Monitor individual performance
After — Promoted Closet
Simple, seller-first
  • Set one daily budget
  • Whole closet auto-promoted
  • Pay only per click
  • Relevance handled by platform
  • Dashboard shows results
Senior design insight

The market-standard pattern wasn't wrong — it was wrong for our user. Knowing when to throw away competitor-research-backed precedent and design for the actual person in front of you is the hardest call to make as a designer. This decision defined every UX direction for the next 3 years.

03
MVP Design & Validation

Designing the first web MVP

We launched on web first — Pro sellers were more active there, and the platform allowed faster experimentation before constraining to mobile. I led the end-to-end UX: onboarding, budget setup, payment flow, dashboard, reporting, and seller education.

The biggest challenge was introducing ad concepts without alienating everyday sellers. Through moderated usability testing, we uncovered three recurring issues:

What usability testing revealed
  • Ad jargon alienated sellers — "CPM," "impressions," and "ad slots" made them feel they were in the wrong product
  • Content density caused drop-off — too much text above the fold before they understood the value
  • Perceived complexity = perceived risk — visual complexity translated directly into hesitation to spend money

These insights drove a complete language and layout overhaul: replaced ad jargon with seller language ("budget," "views," "sales"), cut overview content density by ~40%, and raised the primary CTA above the fold.

Forced Onboarding — 3 Core Ideas

To build confidence before sellers committed any budget, we introduced a lightweight mandatory onboarding focused on just three things sellers needed to understand:

Card 2 You only pay for clicks

04
Beta Program

Building with sellers, not just for them

Instead of a broad rollout, we launched through a waitlist-gated beta. I stayed close to sellers through a dedicated Slack workspace, monitored social discussions in Facebook seller groups and YouTube reseller channels, and ran continuous Sprig surveys inside the product.

This direct feedback loop directly shaped the product. Sellers told us what they needed — we shipped it.

Seller-requested features we shipped
  • Listing-level impression data — sellers wanted to see which items their ads were actually pushing
  • "Sold through ads" attribution — connect spend to tangible outcomes
  • Yesterday's Highlights banner — a daily dopamine hit so the product felt alive every morning
  • Weekly performance email — passive reinforcement that spend was working, even without logging in

06
Mobile Expansion

The conversion doubler — entry points

After web stabilized, we expanded to iOS and Android. The biggest unlock wasn't the creation flow — it was entry points.

Sellers spent most of their time in two places: their own closet and the notifications tab. Promoted Closet was buried in My Poshmark. Moving it to where sellers actually live — a banner in My Closet — doubled conversion.

The most impactful design work isn't always inside the feature — it's about how users discover it.

07
Research & Roadmap Influence

Shaping the 2026 roadmap through synthesis

After 3 years of survey data, interviews, and Sprig responses, the team was sitting on a mountain of insight but no synthesis. I built a comprehensive user journey map for Promoted Closet — mapping seller emotions, actions, friction points, and opportunities from awareness through cancellation.

Then I ran cross-functional workshops using the map to align product, design, and engineering around long-term opportunities — onboarding new PMs, surfacing blind spots, and prioritizing what could actually move the needle.

Research scale behind the synthesis
  • 371 responses — Active Seller Survey
  • 501 responses — Reasons for cancelling
  • 227 + 120 responses — Reasons users don't start a campaign
  • 11 discovery interviews — initial concept validation
  • Continuous Sprig surveys embedded in-product throughout

The journey map directly contributed to the 2026 1P monetization roadmap. This is when my role shifted from "designer who ships" to "designer who shapes strategy."

08
Buyer-Side Optimization

"Promoted" → "Ad" — one word, measurable revenue

Promoted Closet wasn't only a seller experience problem. On the buyer side, promoted listings were labeled "Promoted." In a design proposal backed by Sprig data, I argued that "Ad" was a clearer, more familiar label — borrowed from what buyers already expect across e-commerce.

Before Buyer view — "Promoted" label
After Buyer view — "Ad" label

The result: significant CTR lift on promoted listings — more clicks, more billable events, more revenue. One word. Measurable business impact.

09
Impact & Reflection

What growing into senior looked like

Over 3.5 years, Promoted Closet evolved from a concept doc into a core monetization ecosystem — spanning web, iOS, Android, onboarding, reporting, growth surfaces, payments, and buyer experiences. I drove it as the sole monetization designer from zero-to-one through multiple growth phases.
