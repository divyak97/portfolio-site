01
Context

Poshmark's first major redesign in 15 years

In 2024, Poshmark - one of the largest social commerce platforms in North America - embarked on its first major app redesign since launching in 2011. The platform had grown to over 80 million registered users, but its onboarding experience had barely changed. New users signed up through a generic multi-field form, skipped directly to a feed with zero personalization, and were immediately overwhelmed by millions of listings with no relevance to their tastes.

My role was to lead the design of the new onboarding and signup funnel from the ground up - an experience that would touch every new user across iOS, Android, and Web. The goal: collect meaningful first-party preference signals without adding friction to signup.

"The old onboarding gave us almost no signal about who the user was. Every new user looked identical in our data the moment they hit the feed. We were recommending everyone the same listings."

- Internal data review, Poshmark Growth Team
02
Problem Statement

Zero personalization at the most critical moment

Poshmark's original onboarding was a linear sign-up form - first/last name, email, username, password, zip code, country, and an optional referral code. That was it. No style preferences. No brand affinity. No size data. After completing the form, users landed on a generic feed that felt as impersonal as walking into an infinite, unsorted thrift store.

The project had a clear strategic focus: attract and retain younger shoppers, particularly Gen Z, who were increasingly choosing Poshmark's competitors. The business needed a reason to stay.

Why Gen Z?
  • Gen Z expects platforms to already know their taste - trained by TikTok, Pinterest, and Instagram. A generic feed isn't a starting point, it's a reason to leave.
  • New users were immediately resorting to search because the feed had no relevance - a clear signal the first impression was broken.

The challenge was balancing two competing goals:

↑ Increase personalization signals
Collect meaningful style, brand, and size preferences to power relevant recommendations from day one.
= Maintain signup conversion rates
Every added step is a drop-off risk. The mandate from product: don't decrease conversion while collecting richer signals.

What the data told us about new users

Before designing a single screen, we dug into existing signup and onboarding analytics, session recordings, and the post-onboarding behavior patterns that Roger's team had been tracking. The picture that emerged was clear: new users were arriving with strong intent but leaving without any signal.

Post-onboarding behavior (top actions in first session)

  • 25% interacted with a feed unit - but with a non-personalized feed, this was driven by recency not relevance
  • 14% went straight to a listing detail via deeplink - deeplink handling was broken for new users during login

The data revealed three distinct user intents at signup: shoppers who wanted curated discovery, sellers who came to list, and browsers who were exploring without a specific goal. Our onboarding needed to serve all three - without interrogating users with a long questionnaire they'd abandon halfway through.

"The community connect step had a 6% completion rate. We spent engineering time building it, users spent 0 seconds engaging with it. We knew what had to go."

- Onboarding analytics review

Competitor Analysis

How the best onboarding flows collect signal without friction

We audited onboarding across resale, fashion, and social commerce platforms to understand where the industry was setting the bar - and where Poshmark had an opportunity to leapfrog.

Depop
Social Resale · Direct Competitor
  • ✓Style-first onboarding - asks aesthetic preferences before account creation
  • ✓Visual photo grid for style selection, feels like browsing not form-filling
  • ✗Limited to clothing, no cross-category personalization
  • ✗No size data collected - recommendations still size-agnostic
Pinterest
Visual Discovery · Adjacent Inspiration
  • ✓Topic selection at onboarding is the gold standard - visual, fast, multi-select
  • ✓Clear value exchange: "pick your interests → we show you relevant content"
  • ✓Minimum viable input, maximum personalization payoff
  • ✗Doesn't need to handle transactional complexity (size, fit, SKU)
Farfetch
Luxury Resale · Premium Positioning
  • ✓Department selection upfront shapes entire shopping experience
  • ✓Size preferences collected early, used across all product pages

The RealReal
Luxury Consignment · Resale Comp
  • ✓Collects brand affinity during onboarding to curate new arrivals alerts
  • ✓Strong value prop: "know when your brands drop" creates immediate utility
  • ✗Onboarding is gated - must complete before seeing any inventory
  • ✗No aesthetic clustering - pure brand list is overwhelming at scale
05
Design Approach

Building a preference funnel that cascades intelligently

The core design challenge was this: Poshmark's catalog spans Women's, Men's, Kids, Home, and Electronics - each with their own sizing logic, brand universe, and aesthetic vocabulary. A single flat preference screen would be an overwhelming and irrelevant experience for most users. We needed a cascading architecture where each choice meaningfully shaped what came next.

"Every downstream selection must be a direct consequence of what the user told us earlier. If they go back and change their department, their aesthetics and brands should reset - we can't let stale choices haunt their recommendations."

01
Landing Screen - Value Before Ask
The app opens to a full-bleed visual communicating "Real finds, real inspiration, everyday" before asking for any credentials. SSO options (Apple, Google, Facebook, Email) are presented clearly. The old experience opened directly to a login form - no context, no reason to sign up.
02
Signup - 3 Fields, Not 7
We split the old registration screen into two steps: (1) First name, last name, email. (2) Username (auto-generated from name), password, and optional zip/country/gender. This reduces the cognitive load of the first screen while still collecting the required data. SSO flows pre-fill name and skip directly to step 2.
03
Department - The Personalization Anchor
Users select what they shop for: Women's, Men's, Kids, Home, Electronics, Pets. This single choice is the root of the entire preference cascade. Selecting Women's Wear surfaces women's size categories (Dresses, Pants, Shoes across Standard, Juniors, Petite, and Maternity ranges) and women's aesthetic vocabulary. Selecting Men's unlocks entirely different size buckets and aesthetics. Multi-department selection is supported - the system unions all relevant content downstream.
04
Sizes - Action Sheet, Not a Form
Instead of dropdown menus, sizes appear in a bottom action sheet per department - a scannable grid of size chips that feel closer to shopping than form-filling. Users can select up to 4 sizes across categories. Tapping "Add Later" closes the sheet without saving, keeping the department selected. This solves the old UX problem where zip code, dress size, and shoe size were crammed into a single overwhelming screen.
05
Aesthetics - Vibes, Not just Brands
Before surfacing 15,000+ brands, we introduce aesthetic buckets: Bohemian, Minimalist, Streetwear, Pilates Princess, Cabincore, Maximalist, Preppy, and more - each mapped to 5 anchor brands. Users pick the vibes that resonate with them. This does two things: it dramatically narrows the brand universe they see next, and it creates a rich style signal for recommendations beyond just "bought Nike once."
06
Brands - Curated, Then Open Search
The brand screen shows three tiers: brands surfaced from aesthetic selections (most relevant), then popular brands (social proof), and a full search bar for the remaining 15k+ catalog. Brands are saved on tap - no "Continue" required for partial engagement. If users selected zero aesthetics, popular brands show directly. Skipping brands does not clear existing selections, respecting any choices already made.
07
Demographics - Era + Lifestyle
The final on-ramp screen asks two lightweight questions: age range (18–27, 28–43, 44+) and occupation type (10 categories from Creative Professional to Student). The copy is thoughtfully framed - "Style evolves with you. Pick your era and we'll match the vibe." - rather than feeling like a census form. Both questions are optional, skippable, and FS-controlled for future iteration.
07
Impact & Results

Turning new users into known users

The redesigned onboarding was A/B tested across US and CA markets, configured via Feature Settings to allow controlled rollout and rapid iteration. The primary goal was to hit a signal fill rate above 50% for new users - a 4× improvement over baseline - without any regression in signup conversion.
