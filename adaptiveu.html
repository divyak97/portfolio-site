Project Overview

The core engine of an LMS was broken

AdaptiveU is a SaaS-based Learning Management System that helps organizations onboard, train, and upskill their employees and customers through custom courses. As the lead designer on the Course Creation feature, I owned the end-to-end redesign of the course builder as part of a larger platform revamp.

The existing experience was a major bottleneck. Instructors struggled to build courses, leading to abandoned drafts, low content volume, and poor retention. Course creation is the core engine of an LMS — when instructors can't build efficiently, fewer trainings are delivered, learners get less value, and retention drops.

When the post-pandemic surge brought a significant spike in sign-ups, course conversion rates sat under 20%. Users were starting courses and never finishing them.

Before Existing course builder — high friction, high drop-off
02
The Problem

Why instructors gave up before publishing

The vicious cycle was clear: low content supply led to lower learner engagement, which hurt retention and platform adoption. Instructors needed to create courses to deliver value — but the tool was getting in their way at every step.

01
No way to easily create multiple lessons or steps
02
Rearranging content was a major, recurring frustration
03
Rigid structure with high cognitive load from the start
04
No flexibility for different content types — video, files, text, quizzes
05
No autosave — lost progress killed trust and momentum
06
Multiple setup steps required before users could add any content
The design goal

Design an intuitive, flexible course builder that reduces cognitive load, supports fast content addition and rearrangement, includes autosave, and helps instructors complete and publish courses faster.

03
Research & Discovery

Finding the truth in the data

I combined quantitative and qualitative methods to triangulate where the experience was breaking down — and why.

Research methods used
  • Session recordings & analytics — Hotjar and FullStory confirmed high abandonment points and the exact steps where users dropped off
  • Support chat analysis — ChatSupport transcripts revealed recurring pain around content uploads, rearrangement, and data loss without autosave
  • User interviews — Instructors consistently said they wanted to "jump in and start building" rather than follow a rigid wizard
  • Competitive analysis — Studied Coursera, Udemy, TalentLMS, and Docebo for best practices in content addition, drag-and-drop, and editor UX
  • Internal team synthesis — Aligned with the broader platform redesign to incorporate navigation, personalization, and content organisation patterns

Core insight on course structure:

A course functions like a book — one Course contains multiple Lessons, each holding multiple Steps (sub-topics), and within each step, various Content Blocks (text, video, files, etc.). This mental model became foundational to every design decision.

"As an instructor, I want to create and publish courses easily so I can deliver great learning experiences and drive real training outcomes."

I started with paper wireframes to move quickly, presenting pros and cons to align the team early. Two directions emerged: a guided wizard (teaches structure upfront) vs. a direct, flexible builder (trust the user).

Early ideation Paper wireframes — initial flow explorations
Exploration Wizard flow vs. direct builder — options presented to team
Key insights from early brainstorming
  • Users should start adding content immediately after naming the course — delay equals abandonment
  • Horizontal content palettes improve scanning and drag-and-drop discoverability
  • Default structures (one Lesson + one Step pre-created) help beginners without limiting power users
  • A persistent outline view in a left panel keeps structure visible at all times — orientation without interruption
05
The Pivot

Dropping the wizard. Trusting the instructor.

Prototype testing showed the guided wizard approach created too much cognitive load for new users — the very people it was meant to help. They felt stuck in a setup mode before doing any real work.

The pivot: shift to a direct-entry builder. Users land straight into the editing interface after entering the course name. All default elements are editable on hover. Settings (thumbnail, description, category) are accessible at any time via a dedicated tab — not a gate.

After pivot Direct-entry builder — landing straight into content creation
06
Key Design Decisions

Every decision tied to a user or business reason

Content block layout
Horizontal palette over vertical list — improves scanning, makes drag-and-drop more intuitive, reduces the mental effort of building a lesson.
Text editor toolbar
Explicit persistent toolbar (not floating) — tested both with users; explicit was consistently preferred for clarity and consistency across content types.
Rearrange affordance
Explored multiple drag handles and icons before settling on the most intuitively understood one from user testing — small decision, high discoverability impact.
Progressive disclosure
Advanced options hidden until needed — reduces initial cognitive load while preserving full power for experienced instructors who want it.
Autosave
Strong, visible autosave was non-negotiable. Lost progress was one of the most cited reasons for abandonment — removing that anxiety was a trust-building requirement.
Layout structure
Left panel for course outline (lessons/steps), main area for content editing — mirrors familiar document editors (Notion, Docs) and keeps structure always visible without interrupting flow.
Final design Full course builder — left panel outline + main editing area
Content Content block palette — horizontal layout
Editor Text editor with explicit toolbar
Reorder Drag-and-drop rearrangement in action
Settings Course settings tab — accessible anytime, not a gate
Autosave Autosave state — trust signal always visible

09
Reflections

What this project taught me

01
Start with user value immediately
Remove barriers to the first meaningful action. Forcing education upfront delays value — and users leave before they've experienced why the product matters.
02
Data + rapid prototyping is a powerful combination
Combining analytics, session recordings, and quick Figma/InVision tests gave us directional confidence fast — saving significant rework down the line.
03
Flexibility with smart defaults wins
Support both beginners and power users without overwhelming anyone. Pre-set defaults lower the barrier; full flexibility keeps experts happy.
04
Involve developers early
Aligning with engineers throughout — not just at handoff — created a cohesive, technically grounded outcome and avoided late-stage surprises.
05
Tie every decision to business outcomes
Focusing on completion, conversion, and retention helped prioritise ruthlessly. Design decisions without a business reason are hard to defend and easy to cut.

Working on AdaptiveU taught me to think like an owner: connect every design choice to retention, adoption, and user success. Simplicity in execution is the hardest part — and the most worth fighting for.
